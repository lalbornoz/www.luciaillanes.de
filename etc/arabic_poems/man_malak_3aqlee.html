You who holds my mind hostage

Man malak ʕaqlī rahīn

ﻧﻮﻉ ﺍﻟﺸﻌﺮ: صنعة توشيح
ﺍﻟﺸﺎﻋﺮ: ﻣﺠﻬﻮﻝ؛ ﻣﻮﺟﻮﺩ ﻓﻲ ﻣﻴﺰﺍﻥ درج ﻧﻮﺑﺔ الرصد
ﻣﻨﻄﻘﻪ: ﺍﻷﻧﺪﻟﺲ
ﻋﺼﺮ: ﻗﺮﻥ ﺍﻟﺜﺎﻣﻦ-ﺍﻟﺨﺎﻣﺲ ﻋﺸﺮ

مَنْ مَلَكْ عَقْلِي رَهِينْ
رِيتْ عَلَى خَدوُّ اليَمِينْ

الزَّهَرْ وَالوَرْدُ وَالسَّوْسَنْ
وَاليَاسْمِينْ

قُلْتُ لَهُ آشْ ذَاكْ عَلَى خَدَّكْ
قَالَ لِي القَمَرْ

قُلْتُ لَهُ آشْ ذَاكْ عَلَى شَفْرَكْ
قَالَ لِي الحَوَرْ

قُلْتُ لَهُ آشْ ذَاكْ عَلَى ثَغْرَكْ
قَالَ لِي الدُّرَرْ

لَمَعُوا تَحْتَ الجَبِينْ
حَاجِبَيْنِ مُعَرَّقِينْ

الزَّهَرْ وَالوَرْد وَالسَّوْسَن
وَاليَاسَمِينْ

Type of poem: piece of a Muwaššaḥah
Name of poet: unknown; found in Mīzān Darj of Nawbatu r-Raṣd
Region of poet: al-Andalus
Era: 8th-15th century

You who holds my mind hostage
how I long for your right cheek

O flower, O rose, O lily
and jasmine[1]

I said to her: what is on your cheek?
She said to me: the face of the moon[2]

I said to her: what is on your eyelids?
She said to me: white bright light[3]

I said to her: what is on your teeth?
She said to me: plentiful pearls[4]

They shine bright and beckon below your forehead
and your streaked marble eyebrows[5]

O flower, O rose, O lily
and jasmine[1]

[1] The poet is describing the person being referred to in terms of flowers, etc. The half-verse forms a rhyming pair with the preceding half-verse pairs at the beginning and the end; regrettably, the English language is lacking in words that rhyme with jasmine and signify or connote hostage, cheek and forehead, eyebrow.

Additionally, the four kinds of flowers mentioned may refer to the four attributes of the person being referred to elevated by the poet: cheeks like the moon like flowers, eyelids of white bright light like roses, teeth of plentiful pearls like lilies, and streaked marble eyebrows like jasmine.
[2] The poet is likening the face and particularly the cheeks of the person being addressed to the face of the full moon, e.g. intensely bright, possessed of such intense light that it outshines the sun and all other stars in complete darkness. The trope of describing friends, loved ones, etc. in terms of the stars, the moon, their daily, nightly courses, rising, setting, etc. is very common in Arabic poetry.
[3] viz. large eyes with intensely bright white of the eye, a quality much praised in Arabic poetry and among Arabs, both signifying tremendous beauty and health as well as connoting, more loosely, purity, and less loosely, virginity; cf. Ḥūriyy.
[4] e.g. bright, white, pure, healthy, flowing copiously like milk.
[5] viz. eyebrows that are streaked like marble.
