Type of poem: Lyrical pieces of Muwaššaḥāt[2] with introductory poem (Istiḫbār)
Name of poet: unknown; found in the Mṣedder of Nawbatu l-Māyah in the Algerian tradition
Region of poet: al-Andalus
Era: 8th-16th century

The morning dawns in my relief and therein birds that praise the Lord
as the nightingale sang: O companion! the running waters that flow freely
are there together with six beauties[3] in the water: we greet you by dawn!

My nights of delight spent in good times may they come to pass for all of mankind[4]
We spent the night in delight, in joy, and in solace[5] by the coming of dawn I'm impassioned by its[6] passing
By God, O morning! You parted me from union! By God, O morning! May you settle my affairs!
We spent the night in joy, I and my love, by the ʕūd[7] and by song, free from need, the Rabāb[8] takes us captive
You reached your desires, rejoice O my heart, than six beauties wealthier, my hope was obtained![9]

Why, O morning, was I parted from my affair?
By God, O morning! You parted me from union!
O morning, may you settle my affairs!

O attendant, the time of parting has come and gone[10] gaze upon the garden, see how it rises and falls!
And the blossoming flowers[11] are feigning in pretence[12]: bounteous wallflower[13], slandering rose[14], chamomile calm[15]
The lilies are unfolding, as my mind takes captive my soul and slumber in the company of drum and bell[16]
O attendant, may the glass gladden me, take all that's in it, fill it with wine blended[17], as we blend it!

And together with me, the full moon in darkness[18], takes captive my soul
All that whom see it, their hearts are aroused
All that whom see it, their hearts are astir

Daybreak bore radiance[19] rise, O attendant! let the cups be revolved
and the birds are there singing from beneath the thicket of a fig tree[20]
My cup is radiant with splendour[21] from sweet wine that refreshes the souls
Pour me a drink O gazelle Ah, O my patron fill my cup with wine
Long-lasting wine I drink is sweeter amidst radiant roses and garlands

The morning has extended its light from its east is lifted its veil
And the garden grew in its beauty towards our gathering, O my patron!
Let blended wine revolve, incline it towards our vigil, awaken us to satisfaction
A cup of strong wine, O my patron ...
... ...
[1] The source text is taken from a songbook titled "فاح الزهر فاح", which unfortunately contains a significant number of errors and some seemingly genuine, in part documented, Andalusian orthographical peculiarities, namely:
1) وا- as desinence of مضارع verbs that neither act as adverbial adjuncts nor are subordinated by themselves in meaning (e.g. verbs of desire, wish, hope, possibility, etc.) or governed thusly by a particle and/or syntax.
2) و- desinence of nouns of e.g. roots that are biradical in origin with an epenthetic pseudo-vowel or geminated radical, though in this case this is limited to nouns of quadriliteral roots; this is documented in "A Descriptive and Comparative Grammar of Andalusi Arabic".
3) Nouns in a جار ومجرور construction that are not governed by the genitive case and instead assume the nominative or accusative; this is also seen in this excerpt of a Muwaššaḥah in the Moroccan tradition: You that took my mind hostage

A rendition of these poems by the late al-Ḥājj Moḥammed aṭ-Ṭāhar al-Fergānī, known as the Nightingale of Constantine and the Sulṭān of al-Mālūf [Classical Andalusian music in the tradition of Algeria] of Constantine, was used as basis for both the consonantal text inasmuch as the vocalisation.

The grammatical gender of both the number and the word counted allow for beauties of either and/or mixed in gender.

[6] viz. delight, joy, solace, and dawn itself.

[8] The Rabāb is the quintessential Andalusian musical instrument.

The rebab (Arabic: ربابة, rabāba, variously spelled rebap, rubob, rebeb, rababa, rabeba, robab, rubab, rebob, etc) is the name of several related string instruments that independently spread via Islamic trading routes over much of North Africa, Middle East, Central Asia, Southeast Asia, and parts of Europe. The instrument is typically bowed, but is sometimes plucked. It is one of the earliest known bowed instruments, named no later than the 8th century, and is the parent of many bowed and stringed instruments.

[9] 1) instance of Iltifāt (shifting from the 3rd to the 1st person) between the preceding half-verse (addressing the heart) and this half-verse (the heart responds.)
2) inverting the peculiar word order from the original Arabic, particularly peculiar to Andalusian poetry:

than six beauties wealthier my hope [or expectation] was [has been] obtained [or reached]
my hope [or expectation], was [has been] obtained [or reached], wealthier than six beauties

as in: the desires of the heart reached have obtained hope exceeding all expectations such as to be of greater wealth than even the six beauties by the water in the Istiḫbār coming with and greeting by dawn after all those nights spent in delight, joy, solace, etc.; this may also refer to there having been six nights vs. six beauties with the former excelling over the latter.
[10] lit. has come and brought; though أتى and جاء are synonyms, the latter involves effort whereas the former does not.
[11] specifically: young, blossoming - generally, but not always, white or bright, brightly shining - flowers.
[12] The three flowers, described as blossoming, that follow in the next half-verse are invoked employing homonyms with great ambiguity, in the sense of one word feigning to be another in pretence as well as both pretending to be flowers while simultaneously feigning attributes thereof distinct therefrom.
[13] viz. خَيْرِيّ - Erysimum: wallflower, alhelí (cognate) in present-day Spanish - given to confusion of /r/ and /l/ common to both Andalusian Arabic, even in its standard register, as well as Andalusian to this day, likely stemming from transmission of Arabic vocabulary through Imaziġen/Berbers, where this is also encountered; this is documented accordingly in Federico Corriente's dictionary of Andalusian Arabic.

The word, as with مُبَهْرِجة and نسري(ن) and بَيْبَنُوج, is of Persian origin, lending, along with context, additional credibility to this interpretation.

Secondarily: if read indeed as خَيْرِيّ, this could also be interpreted as charitable, bounteous, generous, liberal, abundant, etc. If read as خيلي as in the verbal noun of خال-يخال plus -ي, this could also be interpreted as assuming, presuming, thinking of as, etc., continuing the pattern of feigning, pretending, etc.
[14] presumably primarily نَسرين or نِسرين or نَسرِن or نِسرِن (cited as such in Federico Corriente's dictionary of Andalusian Arabic) with the final -ن elided, viz. any of:

1) the wild rose or dog rose (Rosa canina,) agavanzo, escaramujo, or simply rosal silvestre in present-day Spanish
2) sweetbriar rose or eglantine (Rosa eglanteria,) mosqueta in present-day Spanish
3) musk rose (Rosa moschata,) mosqueta in present-day Spanish
4) jonquil or rush daffodil (Narcissus jonquilla,) junquillo in present-day Spanish

Secondarily: نسري as in the verbal noun of نسر plus -ي in the figurative sense of bad-mouthing, maligning, abusing verbally, slandering, slurring, etc., again continuing the pattern of feigning, pretending, etc.

[15] بَيْبَنُوج: a rather peculiar word, presumed to be a diminutive of بَابُونَج e.g. chamomile; being as مُبَهْرِجة in the preceding half-verse as well as خيلي/خَيْرِيّ and نسري(ن) in the same half-verse are of Persian origin, just as بَابُونَج, as well as the context in which it occurs, this would appear to be the most sensible interpretation, though it may of course have referred to another kind of herb or plant in al-Andalus; unfortunately, no such word is listed in Federico Corriente's dictionary of Andalusian Arabic.

The sole other reference wherein this same word is found is in this excerpt from a section of التصوير والتصديق بأخبار الشيخ محمد بن الصديق by الإمام الحافظ المجتهد ناصر السنة شهاب الدين أبو الفيض أحمد بن محمد بن الصديق [الغماري الطنجي المغربي], written in 1466 AH/1946-1947 AD:

ودواء ذلك يحصل إن شاء الله تعالى باستعمال ماء الرند الحار والجلوس فيه في الحمام المرة بعد الاخرى مع شرب ماء البيبنوج حاراً عند النوم وإدخال شىء من بخاره إلى الحلق

And its remedy is obtained, God willing, by using hot bay leaf water and sitting in it in the bathtub several times after another together with drinking hot chamomile water just before sleep and letting some of its vapour enter into the throat.

Secondarily: بَنْج as in sedative or anaesthetic, particularly as chamomile can indeed have this effect; in al-Andalus, though, in this context, more distantly, the word also corresponded to/related to/borrowed from:
1) vinácha + it (little wine)
2) Latin vīnācĕ/ĭa (grape marc)
3) Latin būcĭnum (trumpet (used as a lure) with metathesis,) in the sense of to lure the birds or to be lured.
[16] زُنُزج, in al-Andalus according to Federico Corriente's Dictionary of Andalusian Arabic: syn. with صُنُوج: [basque] drum with bell, unrelated to its meaning in Classical Arabic of harp, all ultimately from Persian.
Cognate documented in Spanish since 1605 AD: sonajero, sonagero, sonaxero: musical percussion instrument of Egyptian origin, composed of a metal frame in the form of an arch or horseshoe crossed by sliding rods generally equipped with cymbals or bells, that one makes to sound. (Vocabulista arávigo en letra castellana, de Alcalá)

Alternatively, considering the considerable ambiguity and usage of homonyms in this poem:
1) and lethargic slumber in the company of dark-skinned [women] - unlikely, as thematically unrelated to the theme of being conquered, captivated, captured, under the spell of something, etc. and as it would constitute a hapax legomenon.
2) and lethargic slumber in the company of darkness/shadows - unlikely, as this symbolical interpretation of does not appear to have precedents in poetry, although it is clear that the scene described takes place after dawn and not at night.
[17] At the time, in Al-Andalus (and elsewhere,) wine was often blended or mixed with herbs and spices (such as cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg, and even saffron,) fruits (such as pomegrenates and apples,) or sweetened with sugar or honey, also as a preservative, though apparently blended as opposed to pure wine was known to cause particularly bad hangovers.
[18] It bears mentioning that the sun, the moon, the stars, their setting, passing, etc. are very common tropes in Arabic poetry in general, metaphorically referring to a friend or beloved one and their departure, loss, longing for, etc.

[19] زَيَّقْ: a peculiar word in conjunction with الفجر: to bear radiance, lit., primarily: to put a collar (on itself) of radiant light or to put a collar of radiant light into the darkness of the night; secondarily, of the opposite meaning: to daub (itself or its eye, e.g. sun) with collyrium e.g. darkness; tertiarily: to adorn or embellish (itself.)
The primary meaning incidentally relates more closely to its original meaning in and from Aramaic.
Notably, زَيَّقْ bears similarity with مُرَوْنَقْ[21] in the following half-verses both in terms of etymology, meaning, and encompassing both its meaning as well as the opposite, which may or may not be intentionally misleading ambiguity on part of the poet (e.g. Daybreak daubs itself with darkness and My cup is turbid vs. Daybreak bears radiance and My cup is radiant with splendour.)

[21] مُرَوْنَق: either an augmented (ملحق) quadriliteral verb or - most likely - stative verbification of رَوْنَق, which is defined in:

1) the معجم اللغة العربية المعاصرة as: حُسْن وبهاء وإشراق (beauty, splendour, and radiance)
2) Federico Corriente's Dictionary of Andalusian Arabic as: éclat, splendeur (brilliance, splendour,) from Pehlevi rōy nēk: belle figure (beautiful figure;) the specific derivation from Middle Persian in the form of a singular noun would appear to strengthen the above hypothesis.
3) J. G. Hava's Arabic-English Dictionary: Brightness, splendour. Wavy streacks [sic] (of a sword).

In the songbook: مرنوق - being as this means turbid, a direct antonym of رَوْنَق, that one may yet think of as etymologically related, this is likely an intentional false trail on part of the poet.
Curiously, رنق as in colour in Gulf Arabic derives from Classical Persian رَنْگ, though this is unrelated.

Secondarily: راق-يروق-روقًا: to be clear (wine,) ه: to excite wonder in, whence also: روقة: great beauty, and especially: مُرَوَّق: porticoed (building,) clarified (wine,) etc. betray a very clear connection, and (etymologically) more distantly: راق-يريق-رَيْقًا: to be rippled (pool). to be poured (water). to quiver. to shine (mirage). (all from J. G. Hava)
